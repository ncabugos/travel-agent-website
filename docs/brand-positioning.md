# Brand Positioning Brief — Elite Advisor Hub

> Voice rules for the eliteadvisorhub.com marketing-site rewrite and all future copy.
> Source: locked May 2026. Owner: operator.

---

## The positioning, in one line

**For the top 1% of travel advisors — and the advisors becoming them.**

Elite Advisor Hub is invitation-worthy infrastructure for advisors who treat their practice as a serious business. The product attracts elite advisors and politely repels hobbyists. Pricing is the floor, not a discount.

## Why this works for a $89 Starter tier

There is no tension. Starter is for advisors on the path to top 1% — early in their independent practice, often transitioning out of a host agency or a corporate travel role. The copy reads "built by and for the world's top travel advisors" — aspirational without being exclusionary. Notion, Linear, and Figma all run this play successfully. The Starter price is a foothold; the brand promise is the destination.

## Voice rules

**Lead with restraint, not enthusiasm.** Top 1% buyers are unimpressed by superlatives. The brand voice should sound like a senior partner in a quiet, well-lit room — confident, precise, unhurried. Marketing copy that reads like a Squarespace template has already lost this audience.

**Specificity over abstraction.** "A Virtuoso-grade website in days" beats "a beautiful travel agency website." "Drafted itineraries arrive in your inbox while you sleep" beats "AI-powered productivity." Concrete details signal that you understand the work; abstraction signals that you do not.

**Show the catalog depth — that is the moat.** Most prospects do not know what a maintained Virtuoso hotel program catalog represents. Demonstrate it. Reference Aman, Belmond, Rosewood, Four Seasons by name. Show the cruise partners. The specifics are credentials.

**Acknowledge the work advisors actually do.** They source experiences, negotiate upgrades, manage clients through crises mid-trip, and build referral networks over years. Copy that respects this earns trust. Copy that pretends advisors "just need a website" insults the buyer.

**Pricing language matters.** Use "investment" only sparingly — most luxury buyers find it slightly precious. "$89/month" is fine. "Setup fee from $499" is fine. Phrases to avoid: "affordable," "easy on the budget," "won't break the bank." These are not the cues a top-1% buyer responds to.

## Forbidden phrases

These have been beaten to death by every Squarespace template and SaaS landing page. Strike them all.

- "Modern travel agency website" — every advisor site claims modern.
- "Unlock your potential" — vague, aspirational without specifics.
- "Take your business to the next level" — corporate self-help language.
- "Affordable solutions" — repels the audience this is for.
- "All-in-one platform" — true, but meaningless to a serious buyer.
- "Game-changer" / "disruptor" — Silicon Valley posture that does not translate to luxury travel.
- "Beautiful, modern design" — show, do not claim.
- "Click here to get started" — write the actual benefit.

## Example before/after rewrites

**Homepage hero**

Before: *"Modern travel agency websites built for the way you work today."*
After: *"For travel advisors who place clients at Aman before the season opens, and source villas through Belmond before they reach the listings — websites that earn that work."*

**Pricing page intro**

Before: *"Choose the plan that fits your business. Upgrade anytime."*
After: *"Four tiers. Every one of them assumes the advisor is the asset. The platform is the surface."*

**Starter tier copy**

Before: *"Perfect for solo advisors just getting started — get online fast with our most affordable plan."*
After: *"A foothold on the platform. Curated supplier programs, your custom domain, the journal pipeline, and a portal that will scale with you. For advisors building toward Growth."*

**Custom tier copy**

Before: *"Premium features for your growing business with personalized design touches."*
After: *"For advisors whose brand already exists in their clients' minds. We align the platform to it — typography, palette, premium modules, the bespoke landing pages your referrals expect — and let you spend your hours where they matter."*

**About page opener**

Before: *"Elite Advisor Hub was founded to help travel advisors build great websites."*
After: *"Elite Advisor Hub was built by an active Virtuoso advisor who got tired of explaining to web developers why a $25,000 booking does not look like a $250 booking on the same template."*

## Visual identity cues (for the rewrite designer)

Restraint. Generous white space. Editorial photography (large, slow, confident) over stock travel images. Type that signals competence: a quiet serif paired with an honest sans-serif. Color palette anchored in deep navy and warm metallic, not the saturated travel-poster blues that every competitor uses. The visual stance is "private club," not "online catalog."

## Touchpoints to rewrite

In priority order:

1. **Homepage hero + value proposition** — first impression carries the most weight.
2. **Pricing page** — currently shows old $79/$149/$299 numbers and reads transactional. Needs the locked tiers AND the new voice.
3. **About / Why EAH** — explain the moat (catalog depth, content pipeline, advisor-first) in plain terms.
4. **Schedule consultation page** — copy here qualifies Custom/Agency leads; the right copy filters for serious buyers.
5. **Footer + nav microcopy** — small touches that signal restraint vs. effort.
6. **Email templates** (welcome, onboarding nudges, dunning) — extend the voice consistently.

## How to apply this in future sessions

When writing new marketing copy, journal posts that appear on the marketing site, or supplier-pitch decks, route the draft through this brief. The voice test: would a senior advisor at a $5M-revenue agency read this copy and respect the platform, or would they roll their eyes? If unsure, ask the operator.
